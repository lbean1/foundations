Overview
Whose stories get carried forward?

This is the longest and most technically ambitious unit of the year. Students research folklore from their own communities — through peer interviews, community mapping, and personal memory — and then translate a chosen story into a drypoint etching. The unit is rooted in oral history as a legitimate and often marginalized form of knowledge, and treats students as both bearers and preservers of living tradition.

Drypoint printmaking is introduced as a medium that rewards deliberate mark-making: unlike drawing, you can't erase, you can't blend, and you can't fill solid areas. Every value must be built through line density and direction. This constraint is a feature, not a limitation — it teaches students to think in marks before they commit them.

The unit also carries the Waitsgiving Do Now thread — a recurring narrative told through Fishboys' album "Waitsgiving", one song per class, that provides both a daily ritual and a model of how stories interconnect across a community.

Key Structural Insight

The oral history exchange on Day 2 is the most important single class of the unit. Students interview each other and hand off their written notes — they receive back a document of stories from their own life, already transcribed by someone else. This externalization makes the stories feel more real and more worthy of depicting. Don't rush it or cut it.

Do Now Thread
Waitsgiving

Every class begins the same way: the next chronological song plays, and students draw a slow spiral for the duration of the song. The spiral is not assessed — it's a ritual that marks the transition into the studio. After the song, the teacher adds one sentence to the ongoing Waitsgiving story on the board.

The story was used last year and became repetitive by the end of the unit. For next year: consider varying the entry activity on some days — the story thread works best when it has narrative momentum and is not the same every single class.

The Waitsgiving Story — Running Thread
Different people, same town, all waiting
Song 1 — Waitsgiving Founder
In 1997, someone found a time capsule that said "wait" with her birthday on it and created Waitsgiving Day — a holiday about patience.
Song 2 — The Greatness Waitress
A record store worker in a band, waiting for the perfect musical idea. Found inspiration in a stranger's diary tape, wrote an album, and sold twelve copies. Now she's waiting for new inspiration.
Song 3 — The Driver Choreographer
A delivery driver — the Waitress's sibling — who records their routes and dreams of turning everyday moments into choreographed dance.
Pattern: different people, same town, all waiting for inspiration — and finding it in unexpected places. Each character is connected to the others without knowing it.

Technical Content
Drypoint Etching — What Works and What Doesn't

Drypoint is scratching lines into a plastic plate with a sharp tool. Ink gets trapped in the scratches, the surface is wiped clean, and paper is pressed onto the plate to pull the image. What you scratch becomes dark. What you leave smooth stays light. There is no smudging, blending, or solid fill — every value must be built through the accumulation of marks.

What works in drypoint
✓Hatching — parallel lines that build value through proximity
✓Cross-hatching — layered hatching in two+ directions for darkest values
✓Contour lines that follow the form of the object
✓Stippling and dots for texture and mid-tones
✓Varied line weight — heavier pressure = darker, wider scratch
✓Building darks through density, not through a single heavy mark
What doesn't work in drypoint
✗Solid fills — no way to create a truly solid black area
✗Smudging or blending — marks are permanent, not movable
✗Smooth gradients — transitions must be built through mark density
✗Large flat areas of a single tone — these print unevenly
✗Erasing — scratches are permanent. Plan before you scratch.
The Plate Rubbing Diagnostic

Before ink arrives, students place their plate face-up on the table, lay paper over it, and rub the side of a peeled crayon across the surface. This creates a low-tech preview of the print. Students diagnose three things with arrows directly on the rubbing: Lines (can you see all your marks?), Value (is there a range from light to dark, or does everything look the same?), Texture (where you carved texture, does it read?). This is one of the most useful formative moments in the unit.

Artist Exemplars
Who We Look At

Artists are introduced alongside the technique they model — not as an art history lecture, but as evidence that the constraint students are working with has been used to make extraordinary things.

Tove Jansson
1914–2001 · Finnish-Swedish
Creator of the Moomins. Master of hatching — built ALL value through line density, never solid fills. Every mark intentional. Lines follow the direction of form. Primary technical exemplar for this unit.
Vija Celmins
b. 1938 · Latvian-American
Obsessive mark-maker. Oceans, night skies, and spider webs rendered through accumulated marks that somehow produce deep atmospheric space. Good for students chasing texture and infinite detail.
William Kentridge
b. 1955 · South African
Printmaking as political storytelling. Work deals with memory, history, and the traces left by power. Good anchor for the "whose stories get told" essential question.
Martin Puryear
b. 1941 · American
Known for sculpture, but his print work shows strong contour line and formal restraint. Useful contrast to Celmins — same medium, radically different approach to mark density.
Phase Structure
Four Phases
Phase 1 Research — Community mapping and oral history exchange Days 1–3 ▶

Students map the communities they belong to — family, neighborhood, cultural, religious, generational, online — and identify what stories circulate in each. Day 2 is the oral history exchange: structured peer interviews where Partner A tells stories (10–12 min) while Partner B transcribes, then switch. Students receive back a document of their own stories in someone else's handwriting. This externalization makes the stories feel worth depicting.

Students circle nouns in their interview notes — persons, places, things — and sketch three of them quickly on the back. This becomes the raw material for the composition research that follows.

Phase 2 Technique — Mark-making and drypoint introduction Days 4–6 ▶

Drypoint technique introduced through Tove Jansson's work as a primary exemplar. Students practice directional hatching on three forms: sphere, cylinder, cube. The constraint is established clearly: you can only build value through line density, never through solid fills or blending. Students practice on scratch paper before touching their plates.

Artist exemplars (Celmins, Kentridge, Puryear) introduced alongside student practice so technique and meaning-making are connected from the start, not separated into a lecture day and a studio day.

Phase 3 Composition — Design and plate work Days 7–12 ▶

Students select their story, identify the visual elements they want to depict, and plan their composition. Teacher approval required before plates are touched — check that the design fills the plate, has a clear focal point, uses line only (no solid fills), and tells the story clearly. Plates are scratched across six studio days, with the Waitsgiving ritual opening each class.

The plate rubbing diagnostic happens mid-phase (around Day 10) — students diagnose their own work before ink arrives. This gives them time to add more mark density or clarify areas that are reading as flat before it's too late.

Phase 4 Printing — Inking, pulling prints, and editioning Days 13–16 ▶

Ink arrives and printing begins. Students learn professional inking and wiping technique, pull their first print, diagnose it against their rubbing, and revise the plate if needed before pulling an edition. Prints are labeled in pencil below the image in the professional printmaker format: edition number/total (e.g., 2/5), title, and artist's signature.

The best print from each student's edition goes into the portfolio. The remainder can be exchanged — prints are one of the few art forms historically designed to be distributed and shared, which connects back to the oral history framing of the unit.

Day by Day
Lesson Summaries
Day 1
What is folklore? Community mapping.
Open with the Waitsgiving ritual: a Tom Waits song plays while students draw a slow spiral. Introduce the unit through the framing question: what stories live in the communities you're part of — not in books, but person to person? Students build a community map with their name at the center, surrounding communities labeled as circles. Thinking prompts guide them to consider family, neighborhood, cultural, religious, generational, and online communities. Exit ticket: which community do you think will have the most interesting stories to depict?
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral + story update 7–18 min: what is folklore? — examples and types 18–38 min: community mapping activity 38–45 min: exit sticky note
Day 2
Oral history exchange — the most important class of the unit
Frame the activity before it starts: students are participating in a tradition that has kept communities alive across generations. Review storytelling norms (listen with respect, share at your comfort level, stories stay in the room). Pairs interview each other — 10–12 minutes each direction, Partner B transcribing while Partner A tells stories. Prompts available for when students get stuck. After the exchange, students receive their own interview sheet back. They circle nouns, sketch three of them on the back. Exit thought: whose stories have you heard today? What stories will you carry forward?
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral + story update 7–15 min: oral history framing — whose stories get written down? 15–18 min: storytelling norms + pair assignment 18–30 min: Partner A tells, Partner B transcribes 30–42 min: switch — Partner B tells, Partner A transcribes 42–45 min: receive your sheet back, circle nouns, exit thought
Day 3
Story selection and noun sketching
Students review their community maps and interview notes and choose the story they want to depict. Guiding question: which story has the most visual potential — a strong character, creature, object, or moment that could anchor an image? Students sketch three of their circled nouns in more detail and begin thinking about composition: what is the focal point, what is in the background, what gets left out? Brief whole-class share: who has a story they want to tell others about?
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral 7–18 min: story selection — which one has the most visual potential? 18–38 min: noun sketches + composition thinking 38–45 min: brief whole-class share
Day 4
What is printmaking? Drypoint introduction + mark-making practice
Introduce drypoint through Tove Jansson's work: look at hatching, cross-hatching, line density, directional marks. Establish the core constraint clearly — you build value through line density, not through filling or blending. Students practice on scratch paper: hatching a sphere (curved marks that follow the form), a cylinder (vertical/horizontal), and a cube (directional based on planes). This is not precious — it's muscle memory practice. Finish by looking at a piece of fabric reference and attempting to crosshatch its folds.
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral 7–20 min: drypoint introduction + Tove Jansson exemplars 20–25 min: what works and what doesn't — the constraint 25–43 min: mark-making practice — sphere, cylinder, cube, fabric 43–45 min: which form was hardest? Why?
Days 5–6
Celmins, Kentridge, Puryear + composition planning
Additional artist exemplars examined alongside continued practice. Celmins: what does it feel like to build infinite space out of marks? Kentridge: how does printmaking carry political memory? Puryear: how does restraint work as a compositional strategy? Students begin their full composition sketch — not on the plate yet, on paper. Design must fill the space, have a clear focal point, use line only, tell the story clearly. Teacher circulates and gives approval before any student touches a plate.
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral 7–20 min: artist exemplars (one artist per day, not all at once) 20–43 min: full composition sketch on paper 43–45 min: self-check: focal point? Value range? Story readable?
Days 7–9
Plate work — scratching the design
Students who have teacher approval transfer their composition to the plate and begin scratching. Remind the class before they start: scratches are permanent. Think before you mark. Students who need more composition time continue working on paper. Teacher circulates throughout — watch for students who are drawing outlines only without building value, and redirect toward hatching inside forms early so they don't run out of time.
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral 7–12 min: quick reminder — how to read value as you work 12–43 min: studio — plate work 43–45 min: intention-setting for next class
Day 10
Plate rubbing diagnostic
Pause studio work for a diagnostic before ink arrives. Students place their plate face-up, lay paper over it, rub the side of a peeled crayon across the surface. They annotate their rubbing with arrows and honest answers to three questions: Can you see all the marks you made? Is there a range from light to dark? Where you carved texture, does it read? Based on the rubbing, students identify one specific thing to fix or deepen before printing. This is formative self-assessment at its most concrete.
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral 7–12 min: demonstrate the plate rubbing technique 12–28 min: students pull rubbing and annotate 28–43 min: return to plates to address what the rubbing revealed 43–45 min: what did your rubbing tell you that you couldn't see before?
Days 11–12
Plate completion + scratchboard mini masterpiece (early finishers)
Final plate studio days. Students who finish early work on a scratchboard mini study from the reference library — these go on the bulletin board outside the classroom. This is not optional downtime: the scratchboard study deepens the same mark-making skills and keeps energy in the room.
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral 7–43 min: studio — plate completion Early finishers: scratchboard mini study from reference library
Days 13–15
Printing — inking, wiping, pulling, editioning
Printing days. Technique introduced as a class before anyone inks: ink the plate, wipe the surface clean in the direction of marks (not against them), press paper firmly, peel from corner. Students pull their first print, compare it to their rubbing diagnostic, decide if the plate needs revision before pulling an edition. Final edition of 5 prints. Students label in pencil: edition number/total, title of the story depicted, signature. The best print goes to portfolio; extras can be exchanged or kept.
0–7 min: Waitsgiving spiral 7–18 min: printing technique demo (Day 13 only) 18–43 min: inking, pulling, editioning (rotating through press) 43–45 min: compare first print to rubbing — what changed?
Day 16
Gallery, written folklore sheet, and reflection
Best prints displayed in a gallery. Students write their folklore sheet — a one-paragraph description of the story they depicted: what it is, where it comes from, why they chose it. This is the artist statement for this unit. Brief gallery share: three volunteers share their story aloud. Reflection in sketchbook: what story from someone else's interview stayed with you? Why? Prints go into binders. Unit closes by returning to the oral history framing: you just kept a story alive that might not have been recorded anywhere else.
0–7 min: final Waitsgiving spiral — end of the story 7–20 min: gallery with prints displayed 20–32 min: write folklore sheet (artist statement) 32–42 min: three volunteers share their story aloud 42–45 min: sketchbook reflection
Technical Reference
The Printing Process — Step by Step
1
Ink the plate
Apply a small amount of etching ink to the plate surface using a card or dauber. Work the ink into the scratches so it fills all the marks. The whole surface will be covered — that's correct.
2
Wipe the surface
Using a tarlatan cloth or stiff paper, wipe the flat surface of the plate clean. Wipe in the direction of marks, not against them, to avoid pulling ink out of the scratches. The scratches should stay dark; the surface should come clean.
3
Position and press
Lay dampened paper over the plate. Apply firm, even pressure across the whole surface using a baren or by running through the press. The paper pulls the ink out of the scratches.
4
Peel and dry
Peel the paper from one corner slowly and steadily. Lay flat to dry. Compare to your plate rubbing — what printed more clearly than expected? What needs more depth?
5
Label the edition
In pencil, below the image: edition number / total (e.g., 2/5), then the title of the story depicted, then your signature. This is professional printmaker convention — not optional.

Universal Design Considerations
Engagement
Story choice is fully student-directed — any community, any tradition, any type of folklore
The oral history exchange creates authentic audience for student stories before they're depicted
Waitsgiving ritual provides consistent daily entry point that isn't tied to skill level
Scratchboard mini study keeps early finishers engaged without pulling them out of the medium
Printing as distribution — prints are meant to be shared, which connects to the unit's oral history framing
Representation
Oral history framing explicitly names whose stories get left out of written history — centers student knowledge
Artist exemplars span Finnish-Swedish, Latvian-American, South African, American — not a single tradition
Plate rubbing diagnostic makes the invisible visible — students see their plate as an image for the first time
Composition checklist posted throughout — students can self-assess at any point in the process
Storytelling norms established before the oral history exchange — lowers anxiety for sharing
Action & Expression
Composition planning on paper first — students work out decisions before committing to the plate
Teacher approval gate before plate work — catches problems early when they're still fixable
Folklore written sheet allows students to provide context for imagery that might not read clearly
Edition of 5 prints means the first pull is diagnostic, not final — revision is built into the process
Sketchbook reflections can be written, drawn, or annotated — format is student's choice

For Next Year — Do Now Thread

The Waitsgiving spiral ritual is worth keeping as a unit-opening structure — it signals transition, creates calm, and builds toward the storytelling themes. But running the same story thread for 16 consecutive classes became repetitive this year. Consider either a shorter Waitsgiving arc (8 classes) followed by a different Do Now structure, or building more narrative stakes and character development into the story so each addition genuinely moves it forward.

Appropriation vs. Honoring — Handle Early

Some students will want to depict stories from traditions that aren't their own. Address this directly and early — not as a prohibition, but as a design constraint that requires additional care: research, attribution, and an explicit note in the folklore sheet about the relationship between the student and the tradition. The question is not "can you?" but "how are you approaching it?"
