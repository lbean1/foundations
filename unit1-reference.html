Overview
How do artists get ideas?

This unit opens the year by establishing how artists actually use reference images — not as something to copy, but as raw material for decision-making. Students discover the difference between copying and using reference through inquiry rather than lecture, then apply the three-photo constraint to make an original drawing.

This unit doubles as a baseline read for the teacher: what students choose from the reference library and how they work tells you almost as much as what they make. It also launches the Accordion Book, which will be built across the year as vocabulary emerges in context — not front-loaded here.

Guiding Question

"What is art that is REALLY yours?"

Key Principles Established Here
Principle 1 — Reference is for inspiration, not reproduction
Artists use reference to solve problems, not to replicate. The thinking is yours even when the information isn't.
Principle 2 — For original work, use photographs, not existing artwork
A photo gives you information. Someone else's artwork gives you someone else's decisions. Learn to tell the difference.
Principle 3 — Copying is legitimate only as a declared master copy
Copying to learn technique is a legitimate art practice. Copying and presenting as original is not. Both exist; label them accordingly.
Essential Questions
Questions That Drive the Unit
What is art that is really yours?
What decisions does an artist make when using a reference, and where do those decisions come from?
What is the difference between learning from existing art and copying it?
How do you take information from three sources and synthesize something new?

Phase Structure
Three Phases
Phase 1 Investigation — What counts as reference? Days 1–2 ▶

Students encounter the core question before receiving any definitions. Day 1 uses student work from the previous folklore unit as case studies — two drawings, two different sources, visibly different levels of artistic ownership. Students analyze them before the teacher names anything. The three principles are built collectively at the start of Day 2, not delivered.

Phase 2 Constraint — The three-photo drawing Days 2–5 ▶

Students choose one photo from the reference library on Day 2 and begin a pencil sketch with the constraint: make at least 3 intentional changes from what you see, tracked on a sticky note. Days 3–5 are studio time. Work is kept small this year — a deliberate shift from last year's very large format. Three photos synthesized into one is the constraint that makes copying structurally impossible.

Three-Photo Constraint

Requiring students to incorporate elements from three different reference images rather than choosing one makes copying structurally impossible and tends to produce stronger, more inventive results. Confirm as a keeper for all future units involving reference work.

Phase 3 Reflection — Now I Think Day 6 ▶

Students complete a Now I Think exit card (Project Zero routine) comparing their understanding of "using reference" from Day 1 to the end of the unit. Finished drawings shared in a brief gallery walk — low stakes, no formal critique. Teacher reads the room: what did students choose, what did they change, what does the work reveal about skill baseline?

Day by Day
Lesson Summaries
Day 1
What did the artist take from the photo?
Open with the incomplete sentence: "Using a reference image in art means…" — students write silently, no sharing yet. Show two student drawings side by side with their source images (from the previous year's folklore project). Students use See/Think/Wonder on the pair: what did each artist take, what did they change, which feels more like the artist's own work? Close with the Now I Think setup — students flip their sticky note and write a revised sentence. Stick to the board on the way out.
0–5 min: sentence prompt (silent write) 5–20 min: look at the two student examples — See/Think/Wonder 20–35 min: your turn — combine three references into one piece, any media 35–43 min: Now I Think exit ticket
Day 2
Reference in the Wild — master copy vs. photo reference
Build the three principles together as a class — slides are blank, filled in as students generate the ideas. Then establish the distinction between a master copy (learning technical skills by reproducing another artist's solved problem) and photo reference (using a photograph as raw material for conceptual decisions). Discuss: does drawing a cartoon character count as a master copy? Not always — show the difference between original work based on a character and a true copy. Credit conventions introduced here. Students then enter the reference library, choose one photo, and begin a pencil sketch with the 3-intentional-changes constraint. Track changes on a sticky note.
0–12 min: build the three principles together 12–22 min: master copy vs. photo reference — examples and distinction 22–30 min: credit conventions — "based on the work of [artist]" 30–43 min: browse reference library, choose photo, begin pencil sketch
Days 3–4
Studio — Pencil sketch development
Full studio days. Students continue their drawing, incorporating elements from their chosen references. Teacher circulates: "What did you change? Why?" — redirecting the question back to the student's decision-making rather than evaluating the product. Skill Builders available for early finishers (Accordion Book cards begin here for any student who surfaces an element or principle naturally — teacher names it in the moment and the card gets made).
0–5 min: 36 Squares bell ringer or sketchbook prompt 5–40 min: studio — drawing development 40–45 min: sticky note update — add or revise the 3 intentional changes
Teacher Observation

This is your baseline read. What students choose from the library (architectural detail vs. animal vs. human figure vs. texture) tells you something before they've picked up a pencil. Note who is hesitant to commit, who goes immediately, who needs permission to deviate from the reference, who invents freely.

Day 5
Studio — Completion and synthesis
Final studio day. Drawing should feel complete by end of class. Students finalize their sticky note: what three intentional changes did you make, and why? If work is genuinely unfinished, students can identify one specific thing they would do next — this becomes the basis for a brief written intention rather than a grade penalty.
0–5 min: 36 Squares bell ringer 5–38 min: studio — complete the drawing 38–45 min: finalize sticky note — 3 changes + rationale
Day 6
Gallery walk and Now I Think reflection
Drawings posted around the room. Silent gallery walk (5–7 min) — students leave a dot sticker on one drawing where they see a strong decision (not necessarily the "best" drawing). Whole-group share: what decisions did you notice? Where do you see the artist's voice? Close with the Now I Think card: "I used to think using reference meant… now I think it means…" Students keep the card — it goes into the Accordion Book as the first entry if they want it.
0–5 min: set up gallery, review gallery walk norms 5–15 min: silent gallery walk with dot stickers 15–28 min: whole-group share — what decisions did you see? 28–42 min: Now I Think written reflection 42–45 min: introduce Accordion Book — this card can be your first entry
UDL Supports
Universal Design Considerations
Engagement
Reference library offers ~400 printed photos across categories — student chooses what interests them
Sentence prompt before any definitions: entry point for all students regardless of prior knowledge
Master copy option named and legitimized — reduces anxiety about copying
Low-stakes first assessment: gallery walk with dot stickers, not rubric evaluation
Representation
Principles built collectively — students see their own thinking become the content
Visual examples (student work + sources) before any verbal explanation
See/Think/Wonder provides structured language for looking
Sticky note tracking externalizes the decision-making process
Action & Expression
Day 1: any media available — reveals preference without pressure
Days 3–5: pencil drawing only — levels the field for a baseline read
Reflection options: Now I Think card can be written, drawn, or annotated with images
Accordion Book card: optional first entry, student decides format
Teacher Prep
Before the Unit Starts
Print reference library photos (400+) — landscape, animals, objects, architecture, people, texture, food, nature, still life — laminated or in clear sleeves
Select two student examples from previous year's folklore project with their source images — one that stayed close to source, one that diverged significantly
Prepare Day 1 slides — sentence prompt, student example pair, blank principles slides for Day 2
Cut sticky notes for exit tickets (Now I Think — before side, then flip for after)
Set up reference library display — accessible, browsable, organized loosely by category (not so organized it feels like a catalog)
Prep drawing pencils and paper — smaller format than last year (approx. 9x12 or smaller)
Prepare dot stickers for gallery walk (Day 6)
Have Accordion Book materials ready for Day 6 introduction
Review Canva slide deck before Day 1 — slides load slowly in some browsers
No Vocabulary to Front-Load

This unit deliberately withholds elements and principles vocabulary. If a term comes up naturally in conversation — a student calls something a "value" or mentions "contrast" — name it in the moment and note it. The Accordion Book card for that term gets made then, not before. The unit's job is decision-making, not vocabulary acquisition.

What to Watch For (Baseline Read)

Reference choice: architectural detail / human figure / animal / texture / food / abstract. Note patterns by class section.

Approach to the constraint: Who needs permission to deviate from the reference? Who invents freely without being asked? Who struggles to synthesize across three images?

Line confidence: Hesitant / committed / heavy-handed / expressive. This is your drawing baseline for the year.
